Before You Go

Essential Travel Tips

Our local experts share the tips they wish every visitor knew before arriving in Bali.

Best Time to Visit

Bali has two seasons. The dry season (April–October) is ideal for outdoor activities, trekking, and beach days. The wet season (November–March) brings daily showers but stunning green landscapes, fewer crowds, and better hotel rates.

Pro Tip: July and August are peak months — book accommodation and tours at least 3 months ahead. May–June offers the best balance of weather and value.

Money & Budget

Bali suits every budget. The Indonesian Rupiah (IDR) is the local currency. Withdraw cash from ATMs in major towns — they're plentiful. Credit cards are accepted at most hotels, restaurants, and shops. Always carry small bills for warungs, markets, and temple donations.

Pro Tip: Exchange money at authorised money changers (PT Dirgahayu has fair rates). Avoid touts offering "best rates" on the street — they use sleight of hand tricks.

Getting Around Bali

Bali has no reliable public transport, so getting around requires planning. Scooter rental ($5–8/day) gives maximum freedom but requires confidence and an international driving permit. Hiring a private driver ($40–60/day) is the most comfortable option for families and tourists.

Pro Tip: Download Gojek and Grab apps before you land — they're the most affordable and reliable way to get short-distance rides in urban areas like Kuta, Seminyak, and Ubud.

What to Pack

Bali is tropical year-round (26–32°C), so pack light, breathable fabrics. However, temples require covered shoulders and knees — a sarong (which you can buy for $2 at any market) solves this instantly. Don't forget reef-safe sunscreen, as regular sunscreen harms Bali's coral reefs.

Pro Tip: Bring a portable power bank — Bali's power can be unreliable in more remote areas. Pack a reusable water bottle with a filter — reducing plastic waste is vital for the island's environment.

Safety Tips

Bali is generally very safe for travellers. The main risks are traffic accidents (always wear a helmet on scooters), petty theft in crowded tourist areas, and water-borne illness from untreated tap water. Drink only bottled or filtered water and avoid ice in local warungs.

Pro Tip: Comprehensive travel insurance is essential — include emergency medical evacuation cover. Bali's hospitals are adequate but serious cases are flown to Singapore or Australia.

Local Customs & Etiquette

Bali is a deeply spiritual Hindu island and cultural sensitivity is essential. Remove footwear before entering temples and homes. Always accept offerings with your right hand. Never point your feet toward someone, and never touch anyone on the head — it is considered deeply disrespectful.

Pro Tip: If you encounter a temple ceremony on the road, stop your vehicle and wait respectfully — never drive through a procession. This will earn you enormous respect from the Balinese people.

Useful Apps

Download these essential apps before you arrive: Gojek and Grab for ride-hailing and food delivery; Google Maps (works offline in Bali); XE Currency for real-time exchange rates; and WhatsApp which virtually every local business uses for bookings and communication.

Pro Tip: Buy a local SIM card (Telkomsel or XL Axiata) at the airport for ~$5 with generous data — you'll save a fortune on international roaming and WhatsApp calls.

Health Considerations

Consult your doctor 6–8 weeks before travel. Recommended vaccinations include: Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Tetanus, and Rabies (if planning rural activities). Use DEET-based mosquito repellent, especially during the wet season, to protect against dengue fever. There is no malaria risk in Bali.

Pro Tip: BIMC Hospital in Kuta and Seminyak is Bali's best international medical facility and has 24/7 emergency care. Save their number: +62 361 761263 before you land.
Cultural Respect

Dos & Don'ts in Bali

Travelling respectfully makes your experience richer and the Balinese more welcoming.

✓ Dos

  • ✓ Dress modestly (covered shoulders & knees) when visiting temples and villages
  • ✓ Wear a sarong and sash at temple entrances — they are often provided free at the gate
  • ✓ Remove shoes before entering homes, temples, and many local restaurants
  • ✓ Accept food and drink with both hands or your right hand only — a sign of respect
  • ✓ Step over temple offerings (canang sari) on the ground rather than walking through them
  • ✓ Bargain politely and with a smile at markets — it is expected and part of the culture
  • ✓ Drink only bottled or filtered water throughout your stay
  • ✓ Support local businesses, warungs, and artisans over international chains

✗ Don'ts

  • ✗ Don't enter a temple during menstruation — Balinese temples have this cultural rule
  • ✗ Don't touch someone on the head — the head is considered sacred in Balinese Hinduism
  • ✗ Don't point at temples, people, or sacred objects with your index finger — use your full hand
  • ✗ Don't climb on temple statues or sit on shrine walls for photos — deeply disrespectful
  • ✗ Don't take photos of worshippers during active ceremonies without permission
  • ✗ Don't use single-use plastics — Bali has a serious marine plastic pollution problem
  • ✗ Don't ride a scooter without a helmet — it's both dangerous and illegal
  • ✗ Don't discard offerings or interfere with religious ceremonies or processions

Visa & Entry

Visa Requirements

Most nationalities can enter Indonesia visa-free for up to 30 days, or can obtain a Visa on Arrival (VOA) for 30 days (extendable once for another 30 days) at Ngurah Rai International Airport.

🆓 Visa-Free Access

Citizens of many countries (UK, EU, USA, Australia, Japan, etc.) get 30 days visa-free entry. Check the official Indonesian immigration website for the updated list.

🏷️ Visa on Arrival (VOA)

IDR 500,000 (~$32 USD) available at Ngurah Rai Airport. Valid 30 days, extendable once to 60 days total. Pay by card or cash. Queue is usually fast.

📄 Passport Validity

Your passport must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your intended departure date from Indonesia. Ensure this before booking your flights.

⚠️

Important Travel Advisory

Mount Agung and Mount Batur: Both are active volcanoes with periodic eruption alerts. Always check the Indonesian volcanology agency (PVMBG) status before trekking. Respect all exclusion zones and follow your guide's instructions at all times.

Riptides: Bali's ocean currents can be deadly. Only swim at patrolled beaches and always obey flag warnings. Red-and-yellow flags indicate patrolled areas; a red flag means do not enter the water. The unpatrolled beaches of Uluwatu and Nusa Penida are particularly hazardous.

Drug Laws: Indonesia has an extremely strict zero-tolerance drug policy. Possession of even small quantities can result in lengthy prison sentences or worse. No exceptions, no leniency for tourists.
